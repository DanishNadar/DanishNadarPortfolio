Why email authentication matters more than most organizations think

SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are the three core standards that let receiving mail servers verify that email from your domain is actually from you. When configured correctly, they make it significantly harder for attackers to spoof your domain in phishing campaigns.

When misconfigured — or completely absent — they leave your domain open to spoofing attacks that are trivially easy to execute and extremely effective against users who have been trained to trust your brand.

For a managed service provider like Technology Transition Paradigm, this is multiplied across every client domain. Checking the posture of one domain manually takes 15–20 minutes. Across 30, 50, or 100 client domains? That's not a security check — it's a full-time job that still doesn't produce consistent, auditable results.

That's the problem I was hired to solve.

What the automation pipeline does

The TTP DNS Screening Tool is a Python automation pipeline that takes a list of client domains and runs a structured email authentication audit on each one.

DNS record retrieval — query SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records for each domain using Python's dns.resolver, handling all standard selector and subdomain patterns.
Parsing and normalization — raw DNS records are messy. SPF records can chain includes, redirects, and mechanism modifiers. The tool normalizes these into a consistent structure across all domains.
Validation against EasyDMARC — raw DNS lookups tell you what's there, not whether it's correct. The pipeline validates against EasyDMARC's API, catching edge cases where records exist but are malformed or ineffective.
Structured output generation — results go to JSON for downstream system consumption and CSV for human review, with pass/fail status, policy level, and flagged issues per domain.
Microsoft Graph integration — findings can surface through Microsoft Graph for M365 enterprise environments where email-based reporting is required.

The validation step is the important one

Early versions of the tool just parsed DNS records and reported what they found. The problem: "found a DMARC record" is not the same as "DMARC is correctly configured."

A DMARC policy of p=none does nothing to prevent spoofing — it's monitoring-only, not enforcement. An SPF record with too many DNS lookups (the standard allows 10 maximum) will cause authentication failures in ways that aren't visible from the record itself.

Adding EasyDMARC validation as a second verification layer caught a significant number of cases where records existed but were effectively non-functional.

It also gave outputs credibility with clients — "our tool says your DMARC is fine" is a different conversation than "EasyDMARC independently confirms your DMARC is correctly enforcing reject policy."

What this pattern generalizes to

The DNS screening tool is one instance of a broader pattern: taking a manual security audit workflow, identifying the repeatable structure within it, and automating the structure while preserving human judgment at the points where it actually matters.

Most security tooling either fully automates (and loses context) or stays fully manual (and doesn't scale). The middle ground — automation with structured output designed for human review — is where MSP operations actually live. The tool's CSV output is designed to be actionable in a morning standup, not a data dump requiring a security analyst to interpret.

Extensions in progress: dark web monitoring integration to flag when a client domain appears in breach data, BIMI record validation, and a dashboard layer making per-domain status visible at a glance.
